Technical SEO Checklist: From Crawlability to Core Web Vitals

By Jordan Lee · January 22, 2025 · Updated June 1, 2025

Technical SEO ensures that search engines can reliably discover, crawl, render, and understand your site. Without it, even the best content and links struggle to reach their full potential. The good news is that you can practice every major part of a technical SEO audit using the RankRocket Digital sandbox site before you touch a real project.

1. Check crawling and indexation basics

Start your audit by confirming that search engines can reach important URLs and avoid low-value ones. On this sandbox site, review:

  • robots.txt at /robots.txt, which allows most pages but disallows the /noindex-test.html URL.
  • XML sitemap at /sitemap.xml, which lists core pages, service URLs, and key blog posts such as this checklist.
  • Status codes for every URL in the HTML sitemap at /sitemap.html.

Use your favourite crawler to confirm that all important pages return 200 responses and that the noindex test page is still discoverable but excluded from the index via meta robots.

2. Audit internal links and broken URLs

Internal links shape how crawlers and visitors move through your site. In this sandbox, most links are intentional, but one is deliberately broken for testing: a link to PPC management services that does not exist.

Run a crawl and confirm that your tool flags this link as a 404. In a real audit, you would either fix the URL, redirect it, or remove the link. Here, you can keep it as a controlled example while you practice prioritising broken-link fixes.

Beyond broken URLs, review:

  • How many clicks from the home page it takes to reach deep blog posts and service detail pages.
  • Whether anchor text clearly describes destinations such as Technical SEO Audits and Content SEO & Copywriting.
  • Whether important pages have multiple internal paths leading to them.

3. Review site architecture and canonicalization

Next, check whether the site’s structure makes sense for both users and search engines. RankRocket Digital intentionally includes:

  • A clear hub-and-spoke pattern for services and blog content.
  • A duplicate-content scenario where a “duplicate on-page SEO guide” page mirrors the main On-Page SEO Guide for Beginners.
  • A noindex page that still receives internal links so you can test how “follow but noindex” behaves.

Check that canonical tags point to the preferred versions of pages and that you are not accidentally canonicalising away content you care about.

4. Evaluate Core Web Vitals and performance

Technical SEO now includes performance as a first-class concern. Use Lighthouse, PageSpeed Insights, or WebPageTest to compare:

  • The home page and large-image-heavy pages like this article, which intentionally load big hero images from Pexels.
  • Leaner templates, such as certain service pages, where you can experiment with lazy loading and script deferral.
  • The impact of inline critical CSS and render-blocking scripts in the head.

Pay particular attention to Largest Contentful Paint (LCP) and Interaction to Next Paint (INP). Document how compressing images or deferring non-critical JavaScript changes those metrics.

5. Inspect JavaScript behavior and rendering

Modern SEO often involves some level of JavaScript. This sandbox keeps JavaScript intentionally light, but there are still behaviours to audit:

  • A small script in the head that toggles the no-js class to js.
  • A main script file at /assets/js/main.js that handles dataLayer events, contact form submissions, and A/B testing hooks.

Verify that critical content appears in the HTML source and does not rely on client-side rendering. Then confirm that deferred scripts do not block initial rendering and that errors do not appear in the browser console.

6. Validate structured data

Structured data helps search engines understand entities and relationships. RankRocket Digital includes:

  • Organization schema in the footer on every page.
  • Article and Breadcrumb schema on blog posts.
  • FAQ schema on the SEO Strategy & Consulting service page.

Use Google’s structured data testing tools to validate the JSON-LD on this checklist and other articles such as Schema Markup Explained. Note any warnings or opportunities to add richer context, such as author sameAs profiles or more specific article types.

7. Connect technical findings to business impact

A technical SEO checklist is most powerful when it leads to business outcomes. Once you have identified issues and potential improvements, connect them to goals using GA4. For example, you can:

  • Measure whether faster pages correlate with higher completion rates on the Contact form.
  • Track whether visitors who land on technically cleaner pages such as the SEO Strategy & Consulting service are more likely to engage.
  • Build funnels from this checklist to the Technical SEO Audits service and then to form submissions.

Linking technical work to measurable outcomes makes it easier to prioritise fixes and secure buy-in from stakeholders.

8. Turn this checklist into your own audit template

As you work through this sandbox, capture your steps in a reusable template. Include sections for crawl data, status codes, performance metrics, structured data validation, and analytics insights. The more structured your approach, the easier it becomes to repeat on client sites without missing critical details.

When you are comfortable, move from this practice environment to a real site and adapt the checklist to that site’s specific stack and constraints. The skills you build here—careful diagnosis, prioritisation, and clear communication—are what separate strong technical SEO specialists from the rest.
